# Module 7 — Contracts & Transactions

> **A trusted Realtor does not merely obtain signatures. They help clients understand commitments, preserve protections, control deadlines, document decisions, and move a complex transaction toward closing without losing sight of the client.**

---

## Important Scope Notice

This module provides foundational education about Ontario real estate agreements and transaction management. It is not legal advice and does not qualify learners to draft custom legal clauses, interpret disputed contractual rights, determine title, resolve trust claims, advise on tax consequences, or practise law.

Detailed Learning Experiences created under this module must be verified against current authoritative sources, including:

- The Trust in Real Estate Services Act, 2002
- Regulations made under TRESA
- The current Code of Ethics
- Real Estate Council of Ontario guidance and bulletins
- Current brokerage policies and approved forms
- Current Ontario real estate agreement forms and form instructions
- The Electronic Commerce Act, 2000
- Ontario land registration and conveyancing requirements
- Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada requirements
- Law Society of Ontario real estate practice resources
- Qualified Ontario real estate lawyers
- Current lender, insurer, municipality, utility, condominium, and closing requirements

Forms, clauses, transaction practices, identity-verification rules, reporting requirements, tax rules, and brokerage procedures can change.

Learners must be taught to use current documents, explain rather than merely present them, avoid unauthorized legal drafting, and escalate uncertainty before a client becomes contractually exposed.

---

## Current Reference Snapshot

The following principles establish this module’s reference date and must be reverified during detailed lesson development.

As of **July 17, 2026**:

- Ontario registrants operate under TRESA, its regulations, and the current Code of Ethics.
- Representation, required disclosures, consent, acknowledgements, and self-represented-party boundaries must be handled under the current Ontario framework.
- In a competing-offer situation, the seller’s representative must disclose the number of competing written offers to buyers who submitted written offers, while the contents of offers remain confidential unless lawful written direction and current rules permit otherwise.
- Deposits are significant contractual and financial commitments. Their amount, delivery method, deadline, holder, trust treatment, release, dispute risk, and insurance protection must be explained carefully.
- Ontario registrants and brokerages have federal anti-money-laundering obligations involving identity verification, record keeping, business relationships, beneficial ownership, third-party determinations, suspicious transactions, and certain reportable transactions.
- Ontario real estate closings rely heavily on lawyers and electronic registration systems. The Realtor must coordinate information and timelines without pretending to control legal completion or title transfer.
- Electronic signatures and electronic records may support transactions when legal, brokerage, form, identity, consent, and retention requirements are satisfied.

This module does not freeze those rules in time. It teaches a verification system.

## Big Question

> **How does a trusted Ontario Realtor turn a client’s decision into a clear, lawful, financeable, inspectable, documented, and closable agreement while protecting the client at every transition?**

---

## Why This Module Matters

A real estate agreement can create binding obligations involving hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars.

Small errors may create large consequences.

A wrong name, unclear inclusion, missed deadline, casual promise, unsupported representation, late deposit, poorly understood condition, incomplete amendment, financing change, undisclosed issue, or failed professional handoff can jeopardize the client’s position.

Transactions also move quickly.

The Realtor must often coordinate:

- Client decisions
- Brokerage supervision
- Offer drafting
- Signatures
- Delivery
- Deposit funds
- Competing offers
- Financing
- Appraisal
- Inspection
- Status-certificate review
- Legal review
- Insurance
- Property access
- Amendments
- Conditions
- Closing documents
- Keys
- Final walk-through
- Records

Speed does not excuse inaccuracy.

Pressure does not remove the need for informed consent.

A signed form does not prove that the client understood the agreement.

A condition does not protect the client if its purpose, deadline, process, or fulfillment is mishandled.

An accepted offer is not the end of the Realtor’s work.

It begins a new phase of risk management.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Module 7, learners will be able to:

1. Explain how an agreement of purchase and sale creates contractual obligations.
2. Identify the principal elements of a typical Ontario real estate agreement.
3. Distinguish a client objective from the contractual mechanism used to address it.
4. Explain parties, property identification, purchase price, deposit, completion date, possession, adjustments, inclusions, exclusions, fixtures, chattels, rental items, and schedules at a foundational level.
5. Explain irrevocability and the importance of offer delivery, timing, acceptance, rejection, counter-offer, lapse, and communication records.
6. Distinguish conditions, representations, warranties, covenants, acknowledgements, and general statements conceptually.
7. Explain how financing, inspection, insurance, legal review, status-certificate review, sale-of-property, due-diligence, and other conditions may affect transaction risk.
8. Recognize when standard wording may be insufficient and lawyer or brokerage guidance is required.
9. Explain the purpose and risk of amendments, notices, waivers, fulfillment documents, extensions, and mutual releases.
10. Describe deposit purpose, timing, trust handling, potential forfeiture or dispute risk, and consumer deposit insurance at a foundational level.
11. Manage competing-offer communication without disclosing confidential offer content.
12. Build a transaction deadline and dependency system.
13. Coordinate lender, lawyer, inspector, appraiser, insurer, brokerage, condominium, municipality, and other professional handoffs.
14. Explain the purpose and limitations of title search, closing adjustments, requisitions, registration, funds transfer, and legal completion without practising law.
15. Identify identity-verification, record-keeping, beneficial-ownership, third-party, and suspicious-transaction issues requiring brokerage compliance procedures.
16. Recognize fraud, impersonation, altered-document, source-of-funds, and unusual-instruction warning signs.
17. Protect confidential and sensitive transaction information.
18. Communicate contractual choices without pressure, false certainty, or unauthorized legal advice.
19. Maintain a defensible transaction file.
20. Create an Ontario Transaction Management System and complete a simulated transaction file.

# Professional Mindset

A beginner sees a form.

A developing professional sees clauses and deadlines.

A trusted professional sees:

- A client objective
- A legal commitment
- A financial dependency
- A property-risk allocation
- A communication duty
- A sequence of professional handoffs
- A closing pathway
- A record that may later be reviewed

Trusted professionals do not say:

> “This is standard, so just sign here.”

They explain:

- What the term does
- Why it is included
- What risk it addresses
- What happens next
- Which decision belongs to the client
- Which question requires a lawyer or another professional

They know that a competitive offer can still be a poor offer for the client.

They do not remove protections merely to win.

They help the client understand the trade-off and document the informed decision.

# Transaction Control Model

Every simulated transaction should be managed through seven connected controls.

## 1. Relationship Control

- Who is represented?
- Who is self-represented?
- Who may receive advice?
- Which disclosures and consents apply?
- Which confidential information must be protected?

- Who are the parties?

- Who has authority to sign?

# AI Integration Strategy

AI supports Module 7 as a document-control, simulation, organization, and educational tool.

Learners may use AI to:

- Generate fictional transaction scenarios.
- Build checklists from verified brokerage workflows.
- Compare two fictional agreement versions.
- Identify missing fields or dates for human review.
- Create deadline and dependency maps.
- Classify clauses by purpose.
- Translate contractual concepts into plain language for legal and brokerage review.
- Generate questions for lawyers and specialists.
- Audit fictional communications for ambiguity.
- Create file-note templates.
- Organize a closing checklist.
- Simulate client explanations.
- Produce a post-transaction review.

AI must not be used to:

- Draft novel legal clauses for real transactions without qualified review.
- Interpret contractual rights conclusively.
- Decide whether a condition has legally been satisfied.
- Advise whether a client should waive a protection.
- Predict litigation outcomes.
- determine entitlement to a deposit.
- Replace current forms or lawyer instructions.
- Insert confidential transaction data into unapproved systems.
- Create false signatures, initials, records, identification, or evidence.
- Alter an executed agreement.
- Send client-facing contractual communication without human review.
- Report suspicious transactions directly outside approved compliance procedures.

Every AI-assisted document review must record:

1. The exact source document
2. The version reviewed
3. The permitted AI use
4. Redaction or privacy controls
5. The generated observation
6. Human verification
7. Brokerage or legal escalation
8. The final decision and authoritative record

# Module Case Studies

## Case Study 7-A — The Missing Rental Item

A buyer believes the hot-water equipment is included with the property. The agreement does not clearly identify whether it is owned or rented.

Learners evaluate:

- What should have been investigated?
- Which section of the agreement matters?
- What documents or statements are required?
- What correction may be possible before acceptance?
- What professional escalation is required after acceptance?
- How should the issue be documented?

## Case Study 7-B — The Inspection Condition Under Pressure

A buyer is told that an inspection condition will make the offer less competitive.

The property is older, has visible basement staining, and contains an addition with limited documentation.

Learners evaluate:

- Client objectives
- Risk trade-offs
- Alternative due-diligence options
- Timing
- Specialist needs
- Informed consent
- Why the Realtor must not decide for the client

## Case Study 7-C — The Late Deposit

A buyer cannot deliver the deposit by the contractual deadline because funds are delayed between financial institutions.

Learners identify:

- Immediate communication
- Brokerage involvement
- Legal consequences requiring lawyer advice
- Possible amendment or accommodation
- No assumption that the agreement remains unaffected
- File-note and proof requirements

## Case Study 7-D — The Counter-Offer Version Error

Several offer versions are exchanged. The client signs a document that does not contain the final negotiated closing date.

Learners evaluate:

- Version control failure
- Acceptance uncertainty
- Immediate escalation
- Client communication
- Lawyer involvement
- Corrective documentation
- System improvements

## Case Study 7-E — The Competing-Offer Disclosure Request

A buyer asks the listing representative to reveal the price and conditions in the other offers.

Learners evaluate:

- What must be disclosed?
- What remains confidential?
- What seller instructions may or may not permit
- How to respond without implying information
- Documentation
- Ethical pressure

## Case Study 7-F — The Appraisal and Financing Gap

The buyer’s financing was conditionally approved, but the lender’s appraisal does not support the purchase price.

The financing deadline is approaching.

Learners coordinate:

- Buyer
- Mortgage professional
- Lender
- Buyer representative
- Seller representative
- Lawyers where required
- Extension or amendment questions
- Client decision and documentation

## Case Study 7-G — The Repair Promise by Text

After inspection, the seller agrees by text message to “fix the roof leak before closing.”

No amendment defines:

- Scope
- Contractor
- Standard
- Documentation
- Completion date
- Inspection
- Remedy

Learners identify why casual agreement is dangerous and when legal drafting is required.

## Case Study 7-H — The Unrepresented Party Asking for a Clause

A seller’s representative is dealing with a self-represented buyer who asks the representative to add a condition protecting the buyer’s financing.

Learners evaluate:

- Representation boundaries
- Services and advice
- Required information
- Protection of the seller client
- Appropriate response
- Lawyer referral
- Current compliance requirements

## Case Study 7-I — The Suspicious Source of Funds

A buyer proposes a large deposit through several third parties and asks the Realtor not to identify the true source because “the bank will ask too many questions.”

Learners identify:

- Compliance and fraud indicators
- No-tipping-off concerns
- Brokerage compliance escalation
- Record preservation
- Client communication boundaries
- Why the Realtor must not help structure or conceal the transaction

## Case Study 7-J — The Damaged Property Before Closing

A major storm damages the property after the agreement becomes firm but before closing.

Learners evaluate:

- Immediate notification
- Insurance questions
- Agreement provisions
- Access and evidence
- Lawyer control
- Repair or closing options requiring legal advice
- Client emotional support
- Documentation

## Case Study 7-K — The Missing Refrigerator

The final walk-through reveals that a refrigerator included in the agreement has been removed.

Learners coordinate:

- Evidence
- Agreement review
- Seller representative
- Lawyers
- Replacement or credit questions
- Closing timing
- No unilateral legal promises

## Case Study 7-L — The Key Release Pressure

The buyer arrives at the property on closing day and demands the keys before the lawyers have confirmed completion.

Learners explain:

- Why possession is not controlled by excitement or moving trucks
- Legal closing confirmation
- Brokerage key policy
- Security and liability
- Calm client communication
- Escalation when closing is delayed

## Common Misconceptions

1. **“The agreement is standard.”**  
   A commonly used form still creates property-specific and client-specific obligations requiring explanation and judgment.

2. **“The lawyer will catch everything later.”**  
   The Realtor controls important information, drafting choices, conditions, deadlines, and communications before the lawyer becomes fully involved.

3. **“A financing condition guarantees financing.”**  
   Protection depends on wording, timing, actions, lender review, property approval, and proper documentary completion.

4. **“The deposit is only a sign of seriousness.”**  
   It is also a contractual and trust-handling issue with potential financial consequences.

5. **“A text-message agreement is good enough.”**  
   Informal messages may create ambiguity and should not replace properly reviewed transaction documentation.

6. **“Removing conditions makes an offer better.”**  
   It may make the offer more attractive to the seller while increasing buyer risk.

7. **“The highest offer always wins.”**  
   Price, conditions, deposit, closing date, certainty, documentation, and seller priorities may all matter.

8. **“Electronic signing eliminates document risk.”**  
   Identity, authority, version, completion, delivery, privacy, and retention still require control.

9. **“FINTRAC is paperwork completed after the deal.”**  
   Identity, business relationships, beneficial ownership, third parties, suspicious indicators, and reporting duties may arise throughout the relationship and transaction.

10. **“Closing belongs entirely to the lawyers.”**  
    Lawyers control legal completion, but Realtors remain responsible for communication, access, documents, client support, key control, and professional follow-through.

## Teaching Guidance

- Use fictional but realistic Ontario forms and transaction files where licensing permits.
- Do not reproduce copyrighted forms without authorization.
- Verify current forms and legislation before detailed publication.
- Teach document purpose before field completion.
- Require learners to explain every material decision.
- Include exact dates and times in simulations.
- Create pressure without rewarding reckless speed.
- Require version and delivery evidence.
- Integrate lawyer, lender, inspector, and brokerage handoffs.
- Teach learners never to improvise legal clauses under pressure.
- Include transaction breakdowns, not only successful closings.
- Reinforce privacy and anti-money-laundering compliance.
- Grade the quality of records, not only the final outcome.
- Use current regulator and court-informed examples through lawful summaries.
