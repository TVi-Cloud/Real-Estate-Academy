# Module 6 — Finance & Mortgages

> **A mortgage approval answers what a lender may finance. Responsible planning asks what the client can sustain while still living a stable life.**

---

## Important Scope Notice

This module provides foundational education about Canadian and Ontario home financing. It does not provide personalized mortgage, lending, investment, accounting, tax, insurance, insolvency, or legal advice.

Mortgage rules, lender policies, interest rates, tax programs, government incentives, qualification standards, insurance requirements, and rebates can change.

Every detailed Learning Experience developed under this module must be verified against current authoritative sources, including:

- Financial Consumer Agency of Canada
- Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions
- Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation
- Canada Revenue Agency
- Department of Finance Canada
- Ontario Ministry of Finance
- Municipal tax authorities where applicable
- The Financial Services Regulatory Authority of Ontario
- Current lender and mortgage-insurer requirements
- Qualified mortgage professionals, accountants, lawyers, financial planners, and insurance professionals

Realtors should educate clients about the transaction process, identify questions, and coordinate with qualified professionals. Realtors must not promise approval, calculate tax consequences as definitive advice, recommend unsuitable borrowing, or present a lender’s preliminary indication as guaranteed financing.

## Current Reference Snapshot

The following figures are included only to establish the module’s reference date. They must be reverified before detailed lessons, assessments, calculators, or client-facing materials are published.

As of **July 17, 2026**:

- The minimum qualifying rate for uninsured mortgages at federally regulated lenders is the greater of the mortgage contract rate plus two percentage points or 5.25%.
- Insured mortgage financing may be available for eligible owner-occupied purchases below the applicable insured-purchase price ceiling, subject to current insurer and lender requirements.
- Current minimum down-payment calculations begin at 5% for eligible lower-priced purchases and increase by purchase-price tier.
- Some eligible insured borrowers, including qualifying first-time buyers and purchasers of qualifying new builds, may have access to a maximum amortization of up to 30 years; other insured cases may have a lower maximum.
- First Home Savings Account participation room begins at $8,000 in the first year an account is opened, subject to current eligibility, carry-forward, and lifetime rules.
- The Home Buyers’ Plan currently permits eligible withdrawals of up to $60,000 from an RRSP, subject to current qualification and repayment rules.

These are not universal guarantees. Lenders, insurers, property types, borrower profiles, and programs may impose additional conditions.

## Why This Module Matters

A purchase price is only one number inside a much larger financial system.

Clients may also need to plan for:

- Down payment
- Deposit
- Mortgage-insurance premium
- Appraisal
- Legal fees
- Title insurance
- Land transfer tax
- Adjustments
- Moving
- Immediate repairs
- Property tax
- Utilities
- Insurance
- Condominium fees
- Maintenance
- Renovations
- Emergency reserves
- Interest-rate changes
- Renewal risk
- Income interruption
- Life changes

A buyer can qualify for a mortgage and still become financially strained.

A pre-approval can change or fail.

A lower mortgage rate can come with restrictions, penalties, or features that do not fit the borrower.

A large down payment can reduce debt but also leave a client without adequate liquidity.

A longer amortization can lower required payments while increasing total interest and extending debt.

A property that appears affordable based on the mortgage alone may become unsuitable once repairs, utilities, taxes, insurance, transportation, or condominium obligations are included.

Trusted Realtors do not sell clients on the maximum purchase price.

They help clients understand the complete decision, encourage proper professional advice, and protect financing timelines throughout the transaction.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Module 6, learners will be able to:

1. Explain the difference between mortgage qualification, transaction readiness, and sustainable affordability.
2. Describe income, expenses, assets, liabilities, liquidity, net worth, cash flow, credit, and debt at a foundational level.

4. Compare fixed and variable interest structures conceptually.
5. Compare open and closed mortgages, payment frequencies, prepayment privileges, portability, assumptions, and common restrictions.

8. Explain the purpose and limitations of mortgage prequalification and pre-approval.
9. Describe foundational qualification factors, including income, debt, credit, down payment, property suitability, documentation, debt-service ratios, and qualifying-rate requirements.
10. Identify common causes of financing failure after an accepted offer.
11. Explain deposits, down payments, proof of funds, closing costs, adjustments, and reserve planning.
12. Build a complete monthly and annual ownership budget.
13. Explain the financial implications of property taxes, condominium fees, utilities, insurance, maintenance, renovations, and transportation.
14. Identify current first-time buyer savings and tax programs requiring specialist verification.
15. Explain renewals, refinancing, early payout, prepayment penalties, and mortgage discharge at a foundational level.
16. Recognize signs of financial vulnerability and refer clients to appropriate professionals.
17. Communicate financial concepts accurately without promising approval or giving personalized regulated advice.
18. Use mortgage calculators responsibly by documenting assumptions, rates, payment frequency, amortization, and source date.
19. Use AI for scenario modelling and education without treating generated calculations as lender decisions.

# Professional Mindset

A beginner asks:

> What is the maximum price the client can buy?

A developing professional asks:

> What mortgage amount might the client qualify for?

A trusted professional asks:

> What total ownership commitment can the client sustain while preserving stability, flexibility, and the life they want to live?

Trusted Realtors understand that:

- Pre-approval is not final approval.
- Down payment is not the same as deposit.
- Closing funds must remain available.
- Interest rate is not the only mortgage feature.
- Mortgage default insurance protects the lender.
- Qualification rules do not replace personal budgeting.
- Property condition can affect financing and insurance.
- New debt or employment changes can jeopardize closing.
- A low monthly payment can hide a longer and more expensive debt path.
- Financial information is highly sensitive.
- Tax and mortgage advice belong to qualified professionals.
- The Realtor’s job is to educate, coordinate, protect timelines, and identify questions.

# Core Financial Models

Detailed Learning Experiences should teach learners to interpret, not merely enter numbers into calculators.

## Mortgage Payment Relationship

Mortgage payment calculations depend on:

- Principal
- Interest rate
- Compounding convention
- Payment frequency
- Amortization
- Timing

Every calculator output must record its assumptions and source.

## Debt-Service Concepts

Learners should understand the purpose of:

- Gross debt service
- Total debt service
- Qualifying rates
- Lender-specific underwriting
- Insurer requirements

Ratios are screening tools and underwriting inputs. They are not complete measures of personal affordability.

## Loan-to-Value Concept

Loan-to-value compares the mortgage amount with the property value used by the lender.

Learners must understand that the lender’s accepted value may differ from the purchase price.

## Equity Concept

Equity is the owner’s financial interest after secured debt and relevant transaction realities are considered.

Equity is not automatically available cash.

AI must not be used to:

- Quote a guaranteed rate
- Select a mortgage for a real client
- Calculate definitive tax consequences
- Replace a licensed mortgage professional
- Replace legal or accounting advice
- Process real client financial data in unapproved systems
- Fabricate program eligibility
- Treat old rules as current
- Conceal uncertainty behind precise-looking numbers

Every AI-assisted financial model must include:

1. Fictional or properly protected data
2. Input assumptions
3. Calculation method
4. Source date
5. Known exclusions
6. Professional verification requirement
7. A statement that approval and suitability are determined elsewhere

## Common Misconceptions

1. **“Pre-approved means approved.”**  
   Final approval may depend on verified documents, borrower circumstances, the property, appraisal, insurer requirements, and lender review.

2. **“The lender’s maximum is the client’s budget.”**  
   Qualification does not measure every lifestyle goal, future cost, or vulnerability.

3. **“Deposit and down payment are the same thing.”**  
   They are related but serve different transaction functions and occur on different timelines.

4. **“Mortgage insurance protects the borrower.”**  
   Mortgage default insurance primarily protects the lender against borrower default.

5. **“The lowest rate is always the best mortgage.”**  
   Terms, penalties, flexibility, restrictions, and client plans matter.

6. **“A longer amortization makes the property cheaper.”**  
   It may lower required payments while increasing the duration and total interest cost.

7. **“Equity is available cash.”**  
   Accessing equity generally requires a sale or approved borrowing and involves costs and risks.

8. **“Government programs are automatic.”**  
   Eligibility, timing, documentation, and tax treatment require current verification.

9. **“Realtors should calculate what clients can afford.”**  
   Realtors may support education and budgeting but should not replace qualified mortgage or financial professionals.

10. **“A client’s financial information can be entered into any AI calculator.”**  
    Financial information requires strong privacy and security controls.

## Teaching Guidance

- Use fictional financial profiles.
- Avoid using live mortgage rates as permanent course content.
- Timestamp every program and rule reference.
- Use official calculators where appropriate.
- Require learners to list assumptions and exclusions.
- Distinguish education from advice repeatedly.
- Incorporate property taxes, insurance, utilities, transportation, and maintenance.
- Include first-time, self-employed, newcomer, investor, and renewal scenarios.
- Teach learners never to pressure clients toward their maximum borrowing capacity.
- Update all qualification and government-program details before publication.
- Invite licensed mortgage and financial professionals to review specialized lessons.
