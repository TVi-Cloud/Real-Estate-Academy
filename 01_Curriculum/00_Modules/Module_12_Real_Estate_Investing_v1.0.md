# Module 12 — Real Estate Investing

> **An investment property should not be judged by a compelling story, optimistic appreciation, or a single attractive number. It must survive complete analysis, lawful operation, realistic expenses, financial stress, and responsible ownership.**

---

## Important Scope Notice

This module provides education in real estate investment analysis, property operations, financing concepts, risk, portfolio strategy, and ethical investor representation.

It does not provide personalized:

- Investment advice
- Securities advice
- Mortgage advice
- Tax advice
- Accounting advice
- Legal advice
- Landlord-and-tenant legal advice
- Appraisal
- Engineering
- Inspection
- Environmental advice
- Insurance advice
- Property-management licensing or authorization
- A guarantee of income, appreciation, liquidity, financing, or investment performance

Detailed Learning Experiences created under this module must be verified against current authoritative sources, including:

- The Trust in Real Estate Services Act, 2002
- Regulations made under TRESA
- The current Ontario Code of Ethics
- Real Estate Council of Ontario guidance
- The Residential Tenancies Act, 2006
- Landlord and Tenant Board rules, forms, interpretation guidelines, and current procedures
- Ontario’s current standard form of lease
- Municipal zoning, licensing, property standards, fire, building, occupancy, and short-term-rental requirements
- Canada Revenue Agency rental-income, capital-gains, property-flipping, GST/HST, and business-income guidance
- Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation financing and rental-housing resources
- Current lender and mortgage-insurer requirements
- Ontario condominium legislation and current condominium documentation
- Applicable human-rights, privacy, accessibility, consumer-protection, and anti-discrimination requirements
- Qualified lawyers, accountants, mortgage professionals, appraisers, inspectors, engineers, property managers, insurance professionals, planners, and contractors

Real estate investing can produce losses, negative cash flow, legal disputes, capital calls, vacancies, uninsured damage, tax liabilities, and illiquidity.

Learners must never present a model as a promise.

## Current Reference Snapshot

The following principles establish this module’s reference date and must be reverified during detailed lesson development.

As of **July 17, 2026**:

- Ontario’s Residential Tenancies Act applies to most private residential rental units, including many houses, apartments, condominium units, and secondary suites. Exemptions and special cases require current legal verification.
- Ontario uses a standard form of lease for most private residential tenancies covered by the Act.
- Landlords may recover possession only through lawful grounds and processes. A purchase, sale, renovation plan, or owner preference does not itself create an automatic right to remove a tenant.
- Rent regulation, exemptions, increase rules, notices, deposits, maintenance duties, entry rights, assignment, subletting, termination, and Landlord and Tenant Board processes must be verified for the specific tenancy.
- Rental property owners must report rental income and expenses for tax purposes. CRA Form T776 is commonly used by individuals and co-owners to calculate rental income and expenses.
- The tax treatment of a property sale depends on facts, intention, use, ownership, and current tax law. A profit may be treated as business income rather than a capital gain in flipping or business-like circumstances.
- Canada’s residential property flipping rule may deem a gain on certain residential property sold within the applicable holding period to be business income, subject to current law and qualifying life-event exceptions.
- Mortgage underwriting for investment property differs by lender, borrower, property type, occupancy, unit count, income treatment, and insurer.
- CMHC currently offers separate mortgage-loan-insurance frameworks for qualifying two-to-four-unit income properties and for larger multi-unit rental housing.
- Multi-unit programs may use property income, debt-service coverage, lending value, affordability, accessibility, climate, construction, and operational criteria that differ materially from ordinary homebuyer financing.
- Short-term-rental, rooming-house, additional-residential-unit, licensing, parking, fire, and zoning rules vary by municipality and may change.
- A Realtor’s investment analysis does not replace the independent work of lenders, appraisers, accountants, lawyers, inspectors, or property managers.

The module uses these principles to teach verification, not to freeze requirements permanently.

## Why This Module Matters

Real estate investment is often marketed through simplified claims:

- “The tenant pays the mortgage.”
- “Property always goes up.”
- “You cannot lose with land.”
- “It only needs cosmetic work.”
- “The basement can be rented.”
- “The rent will cover everything.”
- “You can always refinance.”
- “The tax write-offs make it profitable.”
- “It is passive income.”
- “Just repeat the strategy.”

Each statement can hide major assumptions.

An investment may be affected by:

- Purchase price
- Financing
- Appraisal
- Interest rate
- Down payment
- Closing costs
- Rent
- Vacancy
- Non-payment
- Turnover
- Utilities
- Taxes
- Insurance
- Condominium fees
- Maintenance
- Capital replacements
- Property management
- Legal costs
- Licensing
- Building condition
- Tenant rights
- Renovation delays
- Permit requirements
- Market liquidity
- Tax treatment
- Selling costs
- Concentration risk
- Personal capacity

A property can appreciate and still be a poor investment.

A property can generate positive cash flow and still expose the owner to unacceptable risk.

A lawful, well-maintained rental with modest returns may be a better investment than a high-return projection built on illegal occupancy, unrealistic rent, or deferred maintenance.

# Professional Mindset

A beginner asks:

> How much money can this property make?

A developing analyst asks:

> What return does the model show?

A trusted professional asks:

> Which assumptions create that return, which facts support them, what could cause the result to fail, what responsibilities come with ownership, and is the investment suitable for this person?

The strongest investment analysis often includes the sentence:

> “The available evidence is not sufficient to support this assumption.”

Trusted professionals understand:

- High projected return usually reflects high risk, an incomplete model, exceptional execution, or some combination of the three.
- Appreciation is uncertain.
- Rent is not guaranteed.
- Tenants are not spreadsheet cells.
- Financing can change.
- operating expenses rise.
- Capital repairs occur.
- Legal use matters.
- Tax treatment depends on facts.
- Selling takes time and money.
- Leverage magnifies both gains and losses.
- Property management is work even when outsourced.
- A model must remain useful when the optimistic assumptions are removed.

# Core Investment Metrics

Definitions must be stated explicitly because industry practices and underwriting treatments may differ.

## Gross Potential Income

The total income the property could generate if all rentable space were occupied and all scheduled income were collected for the period.

## Effective Gross Income

A common model is:

```text
Effective Gross Income
= Gross Potential Income
- Vacancy and Collection Loss
+ Other Property Income
```

## Net Operating Income

A common property-level model is:

```text
Net Operating Income
= Effective Gross Income
- Operating Expenses
```

NOI is generally calculated before:

- Mortgage principal and interest
- Income tax
- Depreciation or capital cost allowance
- Owner-specific financing
- Major capital expenditures

The exact model definition must be preserved.

## Capitalization Rate

```text
Capitalization Rate
= Annual Net Operating Income
÷ Property Price or Value
```

The analyst must identify:

- Which NOI
- Which price or value
- Which time period
- Whether the income is actual, stabilized, or projected

## Pre-Tax Cash Flow

A simplified model is:

```text
Pre-Tax Cash Flow
= Net Operating Income
- Annual Debt Service
- Capital Reserve or Other Below-NOI Items
```

The model must specify which costs sit below NOI.

## Cash-on-Cash Return

```text
Cash-on-Cash Return
= Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow
÷ Total Cash Invested
```

Total cash invested may include:

- Down payment
- Closing costs
- Initial repairs
- Financing fees
- Working capital
- Reserves

## Debt-Service Coverage Ratio

```text
DSCR
= Net Operating Income
÷ Annual Debt Service
```

Lender definitions, adjustments, and minimum requirements vary.

## Loan-to-Value Ratio

```text
LTV
= Loan Amount
÷ Lender-Accepted Property Value
```

The lender-accepted value may differ from the purchase price.

## Break-Even Occupancy

One simplified model is:

```text
Break-Even Occupancy
= Operating Expenses + Debt Service
÷ Gross Potential Income
```

This definition must be adjusted if other income, reserves, or different underwriting conventions are used.

## Total Return

Potential components include:

- Cash flow
- Principal reduction
- Appreciation or depreciation
- Renovation-created value
- Tax effects
- Purchase costs
- sale costs
- capital expenditures

A total-return model is only as reliable as its assumptions.

# Assumption Register

Every investment model must identify:

| Assumption | Source | Date | Type | Confidence | Downside Case | Verification Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Rent | Lease, listing, market research | Current date | Actual or projected | High/medium/low | Lower rent | Realtor and property manager |
| Vacancy | Market and property history | Current date | Estimate | High/medium/low | Higher vacancy | Analyst |
| Expense | Bill, statement, estimate | Current date | Actual or projected | High/medium/low | Higher cost | Specialist |
| Financing | Lender indication | Current date | Conditional | High/medium/low | Higher rate or lower loan | Mortgage professional |
| Renovation | Contractor estimate | Current date | Estimate | High/medium/low | Delay and overrun | Contractor |
| Exit value | Scenario | Future | Projection | Low | No appreciation or decline | Analyst |
| Tax | Accountant advice | Current date | Professional advice | As advised | Different treatment | Accountant |

# Required Scenario Set

Every acquisition model must include at least four scenarios.

## Conservative

Uses cautious assumptions supported by evidence.

## Expected

Uses the analyst’s best evidence-based estimate.

## Upside

Shows favourable conditions without presenting them as likely or guaranteed.

## Stress

Combines several adverse events, such as:

- Higher vacancy
- Lower rent
- Major repair
- Higher interest rate
- Delayed renovation
- Lower appraisal
- Refinance unavailable
- No appreciation
- Higher selling costs

A property that only works in the upside scenario does not have a complete investment case.

## Common Misconceptions

1. **“The tenant pays the mortgage.”**  
   The owner remains responsible for the debt, vacancy, repairs, legal obligations, and complete operating risk.

2. **“Real estate always appreciates.”**  
   Property values can stagnate or decline, and selling costs may absorb gains.

3. **“Positive cash flow means a good investment.”**  
   Cash flow can be overstated by missing expenses, deferred maintenance, legal risk, or underfunded reserves.

4. **“Cap rate includes financing.”**  
   Cap rate is generally a property-level metric based on NOI before debt service.

5. **“Below-market rent is immediate upside.”**  
   Existing tenancy law may limit the ability to raise rent or obtain vacancy.

6. **“A finished unit is rentable.”**  
   Use, safety, zoning, building, fire, electrical, insurance, and tenancy requirements require verification.

7. **“A renovation guarantees value.”**  
   Costs, delays, permits, workmanship, market demand, rent, appraisal, and financing may differ from projections.

8. **“Property management is passive.”**  
   Management requires systems, judgment, records, communication, vendors, emergencies, and legal knowledge.

9. **“A tax deduction makes an expense free.”**  
   Tax treatment does not eliminate the economic cost and requires professional advice.

10. **“Leverage always improves return.”**  
    Leverage can increase return on equity but also increases payment, refinancing, liquidity, and loss risk.

11. **“A high return projection is better.”**  
    It may reflect high risk or an incomplete model.

12. **“The Realtor should tell the investor which property to buy.”**  
    The Realtor provides evidence, analysis, alternatives, risks, and referrals. The client owns the decision.

## Teaching Guidance

- Require spreadsheets or transparent calculation tables.
- Preserve formula definitions.
- Use fictional or properly authorized data.
- Include occupied properties.
- Include lawful-rent and tenancy scenarios.

- Test no-appreciation scenarios.
- Distinguish repairs from capital expenditures conceptually while referring tax treatment to accountants.
- Include lender-value and appraisal risk.
- Include a failed investment case.
- Do not reward the highest projected return.
- Grade evidence, risk recognition, and model honesty.
- Include tenant-service quality.
- Invite accountants, lawyers, mortgage professionals, appraisers, property managers, inspectors, and experienced ethical investors.
- Update tenancy, financing, tax, and municipal references frequently.
