# Module 4 — Ontario Law, Ethics & Professional Standards

> **Trust requires more than good intentions. It requires lawful conduct, ethical judgment, clear roles, accurate disclosure, and disciplined professional practice.**

---

## Important Scope Notice

This module is an educational framework, not legal advice.

Ontario real estate legislation, regulations, regulator guidance, forms, and professional requirements can change. Every detailed Learning Experience developed under this module must be verified against current authoritative sources, including:

- The Trust in Real Estate Services Act, 2002
- Regulations made under that Act
- The current Code of Ethics
- Real Estate Council of Ontario guidance and bulletins
- Applicable federal, provincial, and municipal law
- Brokerage policies
- Advice from qualified legal professionals where required

Learners must be taught to verify current requirements rather than relying on memory, old course material, informal industry habits, or AI-generated summaries.

---

## Module Mission

Module 4 develops the legal awareness, ethical reasoning, regulatory literacy, and risk-management discipline required for responsible Ontario real estate practice.

Learners examine the consumer-protection purpose of Ontario’s regulatory system, the role of the Real Estate Council of Ontario, professional registration, representation relationships, self-represented parties, duties owed to clients and others, conflicts of interest, confidentiality, disclosure, material facts, advertising, documentation, complaints, discipline, and professional risk.

The objective is not to turn learners into lawyers.

The objective is to help future Realtors:

- Recognize legal and ethical issues early
- Understand their professional role
- Protect consumers
- Avoid misleading conduct
- Document important decisions
- Escalate questions appropriately
- Seek brokerage or legal guidance before acting beyond competence
- Build a career that can withstand scrutiny

By the end of the module, learners should understand that compliance is not separate from client service.

Compliance is one of the ways trustworthy professionals serve.

---

## Big Question

> **How does an Ontario Realtor protect clients, the public, the profession, and their own career when law, ethics, competing interests, and real-world pressure collide?**

---

## Why This Module Matters

Real estate professionals work inside high-stakes decisions involving:

- Homes
- Businesses
- Land
- Financing
- Family transitions
- Confidential information
- Competing interests
- Large financial commitments
- Strict timelines
- Emotional pressure

A careless statement, missing disclosure, unclear relationship, undocumented instruction, misleading advertisement, confidentiality breach, or unsupported opinion can cause serious harm.

Ontario’s regulatory framework exists primarily to protect consumers and support a safe, informed marketplace.

A trusted Realtor must understand:

- Who they represent
- Who they do not represent
- Which duties are owed
- What information must be given
- What must be disclosed
- What must remain confidential
- When consent is required
- When a conflict exists
- When assistance becomes prohibited advice
- When a client instruction cannot lawfully be followed
- When the brokerage, regulator, lawyer, or another professional must be involved

Law establishes minimum obligations.

Ethics asks what responsible conduct requires when the rules alone do not provide an easy answer.

Professional standards turn both into repeatable behaviour.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Module 4, learners will be able to:

1. Explain the consumer-protection purpose of Ontario’s real estate regulatory framework.
2. Describe the roles of TRESA, its regulations, the Code of Ethics, RECO, brokerages, brokers, and salespersons.
3. Explain why registration is required to trade in real estate in Ontario.
4. Distinguish legislation, regulation, regulator guidance, brokerage policy, professional practice, and legal advice.
5. Explain client representation, brokerage representation, designated representation, and self-representation at a foundational level.
6. Identify the importance and required content of representation agreements.
7. Explain why services, opinions, and advice are tied to representation.
8. Identify duties involving competence, honesty, good faith, integrity, confidentiality, disclosure, obedience to lawful instructions, and protection of client interests.
9. Recognize actual, potential, and perceived conflicts of interest.
10. Apply ethical reasoning to situations involving pressure, remuneration, personal interests, referrals, competing clients, and confidential information.
11. Distinguish material facts, seller disclosure obligations, property defects, client-specific concerns, and information requiring specialist advice.
12. Identify risks involving false, misleading, inaccurate, or incomplete representations.
13. Explain the professional importance of accurate advertising, records, consent, acknowledgements, and written documentation.
14. Recognize the limits of assistance to self-represented parties.
15. Respond appropriately when a client requests unlawful, misleading, discriminatory, or unethical conduct.
16. Build a compliance-first workflow for client relationships and trades.
17. Identify when to consult a broker of record, manager, lawyer, regulator, or other specialist.
18. Explain how complaints, investigations, discipline, regulatory action, and reputational consequences relate to professional conduct.
19. Use AI for legal-issue spotting and workflow support without treating AI as legal authority.
20. Create an Ontario Professional Compliance & Ethics Manual.

# Professional Mindset

A beginner asks:

> Can I do this?

A developing professional asks:

> What does the rule require?

A trusted professional asks:

> What is lawful, ethical, properly documented, within my competence, and most protective of the client and public?

Lawful practice is not achieved by memorizing isolated rules.

It requires disciplined habits:

- Clarify relationships early
- Explain agreements before signatures
- Preserve confidentiality
- Identify conflicts
- Verify important information
- Document consent and instructions
- Avoid unsupported claims
- Refuse improper conduct
- Consult before improvising
- Correct errors promptly
- Keep current as law and guidance evolve

Professionals should never use uncertainty as a reason to guess.

They should use uncertainty as a signal to pause, verify, and seek appropriate guidance.

- Build update logs for legislative and regulatory changes.
- Draft plain-language explanations for human and legal review.

AI must never be treated as:

- Legal counsel
- The current text of the law
- A regulator
- Brokerage approval
- A substitute for the actual agreement or form
- A source of verified disclosure obligations
- A decision-maker in a conflict
- Authority to reveal confidential information
- Permission to proceed with uncertain conduct

Every AI-supported legal or compliance workflow must include:

1. Source identification
2. Effective-date verification
3. Human review
4. Brokerage or legal escalation criteria
5. Confidentiality safeguards
6. A record of the final professional decision

# Module Case Studies

## Case Study 4-A — Advice to a Self-Represented Buyer

A listing agent represents the seller. A self-represented buyer asks:

> “How much should I offer, and what conditions do you think I need?”

Learners evaluate:

- What relationship exists?
- Whose interests does the listing agent protect?
- What assistance may be permitted?
- What would cross into prohibited advice or services?
- Which information and disclosures must be provided?
- How should the conversation be documented?

## Case Study 4-B — The Seller Who Wants Silence

A seller tells the agent about recurring basement water intrusion but insists:

> “Do not mention it unless the buyer asks.”

Learners evaluate:

- What facts require investigation?
- What legal and professional obligations may arise?
- Can the agent follow the instruction?
- Who should be consulted?
- How should the seller be advised?
- What records should be created?

## Case Study 4-C — The Personal Interest

An agent wants to purchase a property listed through another brokerage and believes their status does not matter because they are buying personally.

Learners evaluate:

- Which personal-interest disclosures may apply?
- What conflicts or informational advantages may exist?
- What additional precautions are required?
- How should the transaction be documented?
- When is legal or brokerage guidance necessary?

## Case Study 4-D — The Direct Message to Another Agent’s Client

An agent learns that a homeowner is dissatisfied with their current representative and sends a direct message offering assistance.

Learners evaluate:

- What existing relationship must be respected?
- Which communications may be prohibited or inappropriate?
- How should the agent proceed?
- What does professionalism require even if the homeowner initiated contact?

## Case Study 4-E — The AI-Written Listing Claim

An AI tool describes a property as:

> “Fully renovated, legally finished basement, and perfect for rental income.”

The agent has not verified the renovation history, legal status, or rental permissions.

Learners evaluate:

- Which claims are factual representations?
- What must be verified?
- What risks arise from publication?
- Who may need to be consulted?
- How should the description be revised?
- What AI review controls should exist?

## Case Study 4-F — Competing Buyer Clients

Two designated representatives within the same brokerage assist different buyers interested in the same property.

Learners evaluate:

- Which representation model applies?
- What confidentiality issues arise?
- What information may or may not be shared?
- Which consents, disclosures, or brokerage procedures may apply?
- When should the broker of record become involved?

## Case Study 4-G — The Discriminatory Instruction

A seller tells the listing agent not to show the property to members of a protected group.

Learners evaluate:

- Why the instruction cannot be followed
- Which laws and ethical duties may be engaged
- How the agent should respond
- What should be documented
- Which brokerage and legal escalation steps are required
- Whether the relationship can continue

## Common Misconceptions

1. **“Ethics means being nice.”**  
   Ethics involves integrity, responsibility, conflict management, disclosure, competence, and protection from harm.

2. **“A client can instruct the Realtor to do anything.”**  
   Only lawful and ethical instructions may be followed.

3. **“Self-represented means the agent can help informally.”**  
   Assistance, services, opinions, and advice are governed by current rules and role boundaries.

4. **“Disclosure only matters when someone asks.”**  
   Some facts or circumstances may create affirmative obligations requiring professional and legal analysis.

5. **“If a statement came from the seller, the agent can repeat it.”**  
   Repeating an unverified claim may still create professional risk.

6. **“AI-generated content is the AI company’s responsibility.”**  
   The professional remains responsible for what is published or communicated.

7. **“A form guarantees compliance.”**  
   Forms support compliance but do not replace explanation, judgment, timing, consent, or documentation.

8. **“Legal compliance is the brokerage’s job.”**  
   Brokerage oversight does not eliminate individual professional responsibility.

9. **“Industry custom is the law.”**  
   Habit, tradition, and common practice may not reflect current legal requirements.

10. **“Asking for help shows incompetence.”**  
    Timely escalation is a core professional competency.

## Teaching Guidance

- Verify every detailed legal statement during lesson production.
- Use effective dates prominently.
- Compare old and current terminology carefully.
- Teach learners to read the actual requirement before summarizing it.
- Use realistic ambiguity rather than obvious wrongdoing alone.
- Require written documentation in scenario responses.
- Integrate brokerage supervision throughout.
- Separate legal education from legal advice.
- Teach both consumer-facing explanation and internal compliance workflow.
- Use current regulator decisions and bulletins as learning material where permitted.
- Reinforce that no AI output should be placed into client practice without verification.
