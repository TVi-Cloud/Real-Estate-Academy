# Module 10 — AI for Realtors

> **AI should expand a Realtor’s ability to learn, prepare, communicate, and serve. It must never become a substitute for truth, consent, professional judgment, human accountability, or the client relationship.**

---

## Important Scope Notice

This module provides education in artificial intelligence literacy, generative AI, research, workflow design, automation, data handling, client communication, synthetic media, decision support, and professional governance.

It does not authorize learners to:

- Treat AI output as law, regulation, professional advice, verified property information, or authoritative evidence
- Enter confidential, personal, financial, identification, transaction, health, family, or legally sensitive information into unapproved systems
- Make representation, lending, pricing, valuation, negotiation, disclosure, screening, or service decisions solely through automated output
- Fabricate clients, offers, reviews, documents, photographs, signatures, market statistics, property conditions, professional opinions, or transaction records
- Provide legal, mortgage, tax, engineering, inspection, appraisal, insurance, environmental, or financial advice through AI
- Use AI to discriminate, steer, profile, manipulate, pressure, or exploit
- Allow an AI agent to send, publish, sign, submit, amend, negotiate, disclose, or commit without defined authorization and human review
- Upload copyrighted, confidential, brokerage-controlled, board-controlled, or third-party materials without authority
- Present synthetic content as authentic evidence

Detailed Learning Experiences created under this module must be verified against current authoritative sources, including:

- The Trust in Real Estate Services Act, 2002
- Regulations made under TRESA
- The current Ontario Code of Ethics
- Real Estate Council of Ontario guidance
- Current brokerage AI, privacy, cybersecurity, advertising, records, and technology policies
- The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act
- Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada AI and privacy guidance
- Canadian privacy-regulator principles for responsible generative AI
- The Competition Act and Competition Bureau guidance
- Canada’s Anti-Spam Legislation
- Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada requirements
- Applicable copyright, trademark, consumer-protection, human-rights, accessibility, and employment laws
- Current tool terms, data-processing terms, retention settings, enterprise controls, and security documentation
- Qualified legal, privacy, cybersecurity, compliance, and technology professionals

AI technology, platform capabilities, legal requirements, and professional expectations change rapidly.

The Academy therefore teaches enduring principles, current-source verification, controlled experimentation, and documented human oversight.

## Current Reference Snapshot

The following principles establish this module’s reference date and must be reverified during detailed lesson development.

As of **July 17, 2026**:

- Existing Canadian privacy and data-protection requirements apply to generative AI uses even as AI-specific policy and law continue to evolve.
- Canadian privacy regulators emphasize accountability, appropriate purposes, consent, transparency, data minimization, accuracy, safeguards, access, correction, and attention to risks involving vulnerable groups.
- Public generative AI systems may retain, process, or expose submitted information depending on the tool, account, configuration, and contract. Sensitive information must not be submitted without an approved legal, privacy, security, and brokerage basis.
- AI can produce convincing but false text, images, audio, video, citations, calculations, and summaries.
- AI-supported marketing remains subject to existing rules against false or misleading representations.
- Synthetic media, impersonation, fabricated reviews, deepfakes, and unmarked alterations can create consumer-deception, privacy, fraud, copyright, and professional-conduct risk.
- RECO continues to monitor evolving AI-supported technologies affecting real estate services. Existing duties involving competence, honesty, disclosure, confidentiality, advertising, documentation, representation, and consumer protection continue to apply regardless of the technology used.
- Meaningful human oversight, risk assessment, transparency, documentation, monitoring, and recourse are strong governance principles for any system that affects people’s rights, interests, opportunities, or major financial decisions.
- Federal government automated-decision requirements do not automatically govern private real estate practice, but their principles provide useful models for risk classification, impact assessment, transparency, testing, human intervention, and accountability.

The professional remains responsible for the work product.

“AI generated it” is never an acceptable defence for harmful, inaccurate, deceptive, discriminatory, confidential, or unauthorized conduct.

AI can help a Realtor:

- Learn faster
- Prepare better
- Organize information
- Generate questions
- Compare options
- Draft communication
- Summarize documents
- Practise conversations
- Create educational content
- Track deadlines
- Detect missing information
- Improve accessibility
- Build systems
- Analyze patterns
- Reduce repetitive work

The same technology can also:

- Invent facts
- Misstate law
- Expose private information
- Create discriminatory recommendations
- Produce fake evidence
- Amplify stereotypes
- Manipulate consumers
- Generate deceptive property media
- Misread contracts
- Recommend unsafe action
- Automate poor judgment
- Create false confidence
- Scale mistakes before anyone notices

AI changes the cost of producing an answer.

It does not remove the cost of being wrong.

In real estate, errors can affect:

- Housing
- Finances
- Family
- Privacy
- Legal rights
- Negotiation
- Safety
- Reputation
- Closing
- Consumer trust

Trusted professionals therefore use AI with both ambition and restraint.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Module 10, learners will be able to:

1. Explain artificial intelligence, machine learning, generative AI, language models, multimodal systems, retrieval, automation, and agents in plain language.
2. Describe the difference between statistical generation, database retrieval, search, calculation, professional judgment, and authoritative evidence.
3. Explain why fluent AI output may still be false, incomplete, biased, stale, unsafe, or outside professional scope.
4. Select appropriate AI use cases based on task, risk, data sensitivity, required accuracy, reversibility, and client impact.
5. Write structured prompts containing role, objective, context, sources, constraints, process, output format, and review criteria.
6. Use iterative prompting, examples, critique, comparison, and structured output effectively.
7. Build research workflows that prioritize primary and authoritative sources.
8. Verify AI-generated facts, citations, calculations, dates, legal claims, property information, and market statements.
9. Use AI to create questions and organize documents without allowing summaries to replace original source review.
10. Design AI-supported client communication that preserves accuracy, tone, consent, accessibility, and human approval.
11. Use AI for education, simulation, content, follow-up, marketing, and operational support within professional boundaries.
12. Protect personal, confidential, financial, transaction, identification, and brokerage information.
13. Identify prompt-injection, malicious-document, phishing, impersonation, data-exfiltration, and account-security risks.
14. Recognize bias, proxy discrimination, steering, unfair profiling, accessibility failures, and disparate impact.
15. Evaluate synthetic images, audio, video, virtual staging, translations, and generated personas for deception and consent risk.
16. Distinguish assistance, recommendation, decision support, automated action, and autonomous agency.
17. Design human-in-the-loop controls for high-impact workflows.
18. Evaluate AI vendors using privacy, security, retention, training-use, access, logging, residency, contractual, continuity, and deletion criteria.
19. Build an AI risk register, use-case inventory, prompt library, verification standard, approval matrix, audit trail, and incident-response process.
20. Measure AI value using quality, time, error, client outcome, accessibility, risk, and review cost rather than output volume alone.
21. Explain AI use honestly to clients where transparency is appropriate or required.
22. Create an AI-Enabled Real Estate Operating System & Governance Portfolio.

# Professional Mindset

A beginner asks:

> What can AI do for me?

A developing professional asks:

> How can I use AI effectively?

A trusted professional asks:

> Should AI be used for this task, what information is safe, what evidence is required, who could be affected, what could go wrong, where must a human intervene, and how will I remain accountable?

The most advanced AI user is not the person who automates everything.

It is the professional who knows:

- What should remain human
- What should never be entered
- What requires authority
- What needs evidence
- What can be reversed
- What must be monitored
- What requires disclosure
- What should be stopped

AI should increase the quality of professional thought.

It should not reduce the amount of thinking for which the professional is responsible.

# The Academy AI Responsibility Model

Every proposed AI use should pass through ten controls.

## 1. Purpose

- What problem is being solved?
- Who benefits?
- Is AI necessary?
- Is there a simpler, safer method?
- Does the purpose align with professional duties?

## 2. Data

- What information is required?
- Is personal or confidential information involved?
- Can the data be removed, minimized, masked, or fictionalized?
- Where will it be stored?
- Can it be deleted?

## 3. Authority

- Who authorized the use?
- Does the brokerage permit it?
- Does the client need to consent?
- Does the user have permission to upload the material?
- Is a regulated professional required?

## 4. Source

- What authoritative information is available?
- Is the source current?
- Is the source complete?
- Can the AI access it accurately?
- Will the original remain available for review?

## 5. Risk

- What could go wrong?
- Who could be harmed?
- Is the output reversible?
- Could the system discriminate, mislead, expose data, or create legal commitment?
- What is the worst plausible outcome?

## 6. Human Oversight

- Who reviews the output?
- What expertise is required?
- Can the human disagree?
- Is there enough time to review?
- Does the human understand the task?

## 7. Transparency

- Should the client know AI was used?
- Is synthetic media labelled?
- Are assumptions visible?
- Are limitations clear?
- Is the professional accountable?

## 8. Security

- Is the tool approved?
- Is access controlled?
- Is multi-factor authentication enabled?
- Are integrations limited?
- Are logs available?
- Can a malicious input trigger action?

## 9. Record

- What prompt or instruction was used?
- Which model or tool?
- Which sources?
- Which version?
- Who reviewed it?
- What final action was taken?

## 10. Monitoring

- Is the system still accurate?
- Has the model changed?
- Has the law changed?
- Are errors increasing?
- Is the workflow still useful?
- When should it be suspended or retired?

# AI Use-Case Risk Tiers

## Tier 0 — No AI

AI should not be used when:

- Law, policy, consent, or confidentiality prohibits it
- The data cannot be protected
- The task requires a regulated professional
- The system cannot be reviewed
- The harm would be severe and irreversible
- The AI use would mislead the client or public

## Tier 1 — Low-Risk Assistance

Examples:

- Fictional brainstorming
- General study questions
- Formatting
- Non-confidential checklists
- Internal templates
- Public-source topic ideas

Controls:

- Human review
- No sensitive data
- No client-facing publication without approval

## Tier 2 — Moderate-Risk Drafting and Analysis

Examples:

- Client email draft
- Property research outline
- Public-source summary
- Content draft
- Meeting preparation
- Document extraction

Controls:

- Approved tools
- Source verification
- Data minimization
- Human approval
- Record of material changes

## Tier 3 — High-Risk Decision Support

Examples:

- Contract comparison
- Property-risk summary
- Financial scenario
- Negotiation preparation
- Client segmentation
- Complaint analysis
- Compliance issue spotting

Controls:

- Expert review
- Original-source review
- No autonomous action
- Documented assumptions
- Bias and privacy testing
- Brokerage or specialist involvement

## Tier 4 — Prohibited or Exceptional Autonomous Action

Examples:

- Signing
- Negotiating
- Waiving conditions
- Sending legal commitments
- Selecting clients
- Denying service
- Reporting to authorities without approved procedure
- Publishing unverified property or market claims
- Revealing confidential information
- Moving money
- Changing executed documents
- Impersonating a person

Default position:

- Prohibited unless a future lawful, brokerage-approved, technically controlled, professionally reviewed process explicitly authorizes the specific action.

# Prompt Architecture

A professional prompt should include the following components where relevant.

## Role

Define the bounded function the system should perform.

## Objective

State the exact outcome required.

## Context

Provide necessary background without unnecessary personal information.

## Sources

Identify authoritative material and require the system to stay within it.

## Constraints

Specify:

- Jurisdiction
- Date
- Professional scope
- Confidentiality
- Prohibited conclusions
- Required cautions
- Length
- Tone

## Process

Tell the system how to:

- Identify missing information
- Separate fact from inference
- Show assumptions
- Compare sources
- Ask questions
- Stop when evidence is insufficient

## Output Format

Request:

- Table
- Checklist
- Brief
- Issue log
- Draft
- Comparison
- Structured data
- Questions

## Evaluation

Require the output to be checked for:

- Accuracy
- Completeness
- Bias
- Unsupported claims
- Privacy
- Scope
- Client relevance
- Need for human review

# Verification Standard

AI output affecting a professional decision should be divided into categories.

| Category | Meaning | Required Action |
|---|---|---|
| Verified fact | Confirmed through an authoritative source | Record source and date |
| Source statement | Accurately attributed to a document or person | Preserve context and wording |
| Calculation | Derived from stated inputs and method | Recalculate with an approved tool |
| Inference | Reasoned possibility based on evidence | Label clearly and review |
| AI suggestion | Generated option without authority | Evaluate independently |
| Assumption | Unconfirmed premise | Verify or remove |
| Professional opinion | Requires qualified expertise | Refer to the appropriate professional |
| Unknown | Evidence is insufficient | State uncertainty and investigate |
| Prohibited conclusion | Outside scope or unsafe | Do not use |

# Human-in-the-Loop Design

A human review is meaningful only when the reviewer:

- Has authority
- Has relevant competence
- Can access the source
- Understands the system’s limitations
- Has enough time
- Can change or reject the output
- Is not pressured to approve automatically
- Records the final decision

A button labelled “approve” is not meaningful oversight if the person cannot understand what they are approving.

## Common Misconceptions

1. **“AI understands the answer.”**  
   Generative systems produce outputs from learned patterns and context. Fluency does not prove understanding or truth.

2. **“A citation means the source exists.”**  
   AI systems can fabricate sources, titles, quotations, and links.

3. **“Removing the client’s name makes the data anonymous.”**  
   Context, property, timing, family details, transaction facts, and combinations of information may still identify a person.

4. **“A paid AI account is automatically safe for confidential data.”**  
   Safety depends on contract, configuration, retention, training use, security, access, brokerage approval, and intended purpose.

5. **“Human review solves every risk.”**  
   Review is ineffective when the reviewer lacks time, evidence, competence, authority, or ability to reject the output.

6. **“Automation always saves time.”**  
   Poor automation can increase review, correction, confusion, and reputational cost.

7. **“AI is objective.”**  
   Systems may reproduce bias from data, design, prompts, proxies, and deployment choices.

8. **“AI can replace a regulated professional if it gives the same answer.”**  
   Professional authority, investigation, accountability, context, and duty cannot be replaced by a generated response.

9. **“The latest model is always best.”**  
   Suitability depends on accuracy, privacy, security, control, cost, reliability, auditability, and task.

10. **“An AI agent is just a chatbot.”**  
    Tool access, memory, autonomy, and action permissions create much greater operational risk.

11. **“Synthetic media is acceptable if it looks realistic.”**  
    Realism can increase the risk of deception, impersonation, and consumer misunderstanding.

12. **“AI transparency means announcing every spelling correction.”**  
    Transparency should be proportionate to materiality, client expectation, synthetic content, automated action, and decision impact.

## Teaching Guidance

- Require learners to work with multiple models and tools where feasible.
- Use fictional or public data only.
- Demonstrate failures deliberately.
- Grade verification, not output polish alone.
- Require original-source review.
- Preserve prompts and versions.
- Include prompt-injection and malicious-document exercises.
- Teach tool permissions before agents.
- Include accessibility benefits alongside risks.
- Use risk tiers.
- Require privacy and bias review.
- Timestamp every tool-specific instruction.
- Retest workflows after model updates.
- Avoid making the Academy dependent on one vendor.
- Include manual fallback.
- Invite privacy, cybersecurity, legal, accessibility, AI-governance, brokerage, and technical professionals to review advanced content.
- Update the module frequently.
